Can chanting "OM" reduce stress, calm the mind, and improve your health?

For millions of people, chanting OM is a spiritual practice. But in recent years, researchers have begun studying whether this ancient sound creates measurable changes in the brain and body — and early findings are worth paying attention to.

This article explores what science currently says about OM chanting, including its effects on anxiety, the stress response, heart rate variability, blood pressure, and the nervous system.

> Early research suggests that OM chanting may help activate relaxation pathways in the body, reduce stress-related brain activity, and support emotional regulation.

## What Is OM Chanting?

OM (also written as **AUM**) is one of the oldest sacred syllables in Indian spiritual traditions. It appears in texts such as the **Mandukya Upanishad**, where OM is described as representing total consciousness.

Traditionally, OM chanting is used in meditation, breathwork, yoga, prayer, and mantra repetition. From a modern wellness perspective, what makes it particularly interesting to researchers is the combination of elements it involves: controlled breathing, vocal resonance, focused attention, and calm awareness. Each of these factors, even in isolation, is already linked to relaxation and stress reduction.

## Why Researchers Are Interested in OM

Scientists study OM chanting because it naturally packages several mechanisms already associated with calming the nervous system — slow exhalation, sound vibration, repetitive rhythm, breath regulation, and reduced mental chatter. This makes OM a valuable subject in neuroscience and mind-body medicine, where the goal is understanding what traditional practices actually do physiologically.

## OM Chanting and the Brain

One of the most cited studies on OM chanting was published in the **International Journal of Yoga**.

**Neurohemodynamic Correlates of 'OM' Chanting: A Pilot Functional MRI Study** — Dr. B.G. Kalyani et al.

Researchers observed participants while chanting OM inside an fMRI scanner. The study reported reduced activity in parts of the brain linked to emotional stress — including regions associated with fear processing such as the amygdala. This pattern resembled responses seen in some relaxation therapies.

The implication is that OM chanting may help calm stress-related brain circuits — though it is important to note this was a small pilot study, and larger-scale research is still needed before firm conclusions can be drawn.

[Read the study on PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21654968/)

## OM Chanting and the Vagus Nerve

The **vagus nerve** is one of the most important nerves in the body. It connects the brain with the heart, lungs, and digestive system, and plays a central role in switching the body from a stressed state into a relaxed one.

Researchers believe OM chanting may support vagal activation through a combination of slow breathing, prolonged exhalation, vocal cord vibration, and throat resonance. When vagal tone improves, people may experience a lower stress response, slower heart rate, better digestion, improved emotional regulation, and better sleep quality.

This is one of the most plausible scientific mechanisms behind why many people find OM chanting calming — it is not merely psychological.

## OM Chanting for Stress, Anxiety, and Cortisol

Stress triggers the release of **cortisol**, the hormone associated with fight-or-flight mode. Although direct large-scale cortisol studies specifically on OM chanting are still limited, practices involving slow breathing, mantra repetition, and meditation have repeatedly shown stress-lowering effects in well-designed studies.

Because OM chanting combines all three elements, many researchers view it as a promising stress-management tool. Possible benefits may include reduced anxious thoughts, calmer breathing, less restlessness, improved concentration, and easier emotional control.

## OM Chanting and Heart Rate Variability

Heart Rate Variability (HRV) measures the variation between heartbeats. Higher HRV is associated with better nervous system flexibility, stronger stress resilience, improved recovery, and better sleep quality — it is one of the most reliable physiological markers of overall wellbeing.

Research on short OM chanting sessions found increased parasympathetic activity and improved relaxation markers, suggesting that even a few minutes may help the body move into a calmer physiological state.

[Read the study on PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35444369/)

## OM Chanting and Blood Pressure

A more recent study explored the effect of listening to OM chanting in people with hypertension. Participants exposed to OM chanting showed reductions in both blood pressure and pulse rate.

This is worth noting because it suggests that even passive listening — not just active chanting — may influence the nervous system. That said, this should be viewed as a supportive wellness practice and not a replacement for medical treatment or prescribed medication.

## How to Practise OM Chanting

### Step 1: Sit Upright

Sit comfortably on a chair or the floor with your spine straight, shoulders relaxed, and jaw loose. Good posture allows the breath to move freely.

### Step 2: Inhale

Inhale gently and fully through the nose.

### Step 3: Chant in Two Parts

Exhale slowly while chanting:
- **"Ooooo..."** — long, open, felt in the chest
- **"Mmmmm..."** — lips closed, humming vibration in the face and skull

Let the exhale be slow and natural. Do not force the sound.

### Step 4: Repeat

Start with 11 repetitions or 5 minutes daily, whichever feels more natural. Increase gradually as the practice becomes comfortable.

## Best Time to Chant OM

**Morning** is ideal for setting a calm, focused tone before the demands of the day begin.

**Evening** works well after work stress — it helps the nervous system transition out of the alertness required during work hours.

**Before sleep** can support mental slowing and easier relaxation if overthinking or restlessness is an issue at night.

Consistency across any of these times matters far more than the specific slot chosen.

## Why OM Chanting Feels Powerful

Many people report feeling noticeably calm after OM chanting because it creates what researchers describe as a full-body calming loop. The long exhale triggers the relaxation response; the chest and facial resonance provide physical sensation to anchor attention; the auditory focus reduces mental chatter; and the rhythmic repetition signals safety to the nervous system.

This is not mystical — it is a combination of physiological mechanisms that happen to work together in a single, simple practice.

## 21-Day Experiment

If you want to test this for yourself, try the following for 21 days:

- 5 minutes daily, at the same time each day
- A quiet room with minimal distraction
- No phone, no music — just the practice

After 21 days, honestly observe any changes in stress levels, sleep quality, ability to focus, and mood stability. Most people notice something shift within the first week.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is OM chanting religious or scientific?

It can be both. Spiritually, OM is sacred in Hindu and yogic traditions. Scientifically, it can be studied as a breathing-sound meditation practice with measurable physiological effects. The two are not mutually exclusive.

### How long should I chant daily?

Start with 3 to 5 minutes, or 11 repetitions. Consistency matters far more than duration — a short daily practice is more effective than an occasional long session.

### Can I just listen to OM chanting instead of chanting myself?

Some studies suggest that listening may also have calming effects. It is a reasonable starting point, though active chanting engages the breath and vocal resonance more directly.

### Is OM chanting safe?

Generally yes for healthy individuals. If you have respiratory conditions, cardiovascular issues, or other medical concerns, consult a healthcare professional before starting.
